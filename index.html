FAQ

Common questions

Burnout is a state of chronic stress leading to physical and emotional exhaustion, cynicism and detachment, and feelings of ineffectiveness. The World Health Organization officially recognized it in 2019 as an occupational phenomenon. It's distinct from ordinary tiredness — it builds over months and doesn't resolve with a single night of sleep.
This is a self-screening tool based on validated burnout research frameworks. It's designed to raise self-awareness, not to replace clinical diagnosis. If your score is high, or if you're struggling significantly, please speak with a doctor or mental health professional who can properly assess your situation.

Burnout is primarily work-related and typically improves significantly with rest, reduced workload, and better boundaries. Depression is a broader clinical condition that affects all areas of life regardless of work context. The two can co-occur, and high burnout can contribute to depression. If you're unsure which applies, please speak with a mental health professional — this tool cannot and should not be used to distinguish between them.
